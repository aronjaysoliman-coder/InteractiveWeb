The Stellar Life Cycle

Stars are born, change over time, and eventually die. Their entire life cycle is controlled mainly by their mass, but their internal chemistry—especially hydrogen, helium, nitrogen, and their temperature—also determines how they evolve.

Birth of a Star: Nebula Protostar

Stars begin inside large clouds of gas and dust called nebulae. Gravity pulls material together until it forms a protostar, which heats up as it contracts.

Key factor:

  • Mass — determines whether the protostar becomes a small star or a massive one.

Main Sequence Star

Once nuclear fusion starts in the core, the protostar becomes a main sequence star, where it spends most of its life converting hydrogen into helium. The star's mass determines its brightness, temperature, and lifespan during this stable phase.

Tip: A star remains in the main sequence stage for the majority of its life, with larger stars burning brighter but living shorter lives.

Fun fact: Our Sun is currently a main sequence star, fusing hydrogen into helium in its core.

How the factors influence this stage:

Hydrogen (H)

  • This is the star’s main fuel.
  • Higher hydrogen content allows the star to sustain fusion longer, extending its main sequence phase.

Helium (He)

  • As hydrogen fuses into helium, the helium accumulates in the core.
  • Increased helium levels lead to changes in the star's structure, causing it to expand into a red giant or supergiant.

Nitrogen (N)

  • Nitrogen is produced in later fusion stages.
  • Higher nitrogen levels indicate that the star is in an advanced stage of its life cycle, often preceding a supernova or collapse into a neutron star or black hole.

Temperature (Temperature)

  • The star's surface temperature affects its color and luminosity.
  • Higher temperatures result in bluer, more luminous stars, while lower temperatures yield redder, dimmer stars.

Mass

  • Mass is the primary determinant of a star's life cycle.
  • More massive stars have shorter lifespans and undergo more dramatic changes, while less massive stars evolve more slowly and quietly.

Red Giant or Supergiant Stage

The outer layers expand, forming a red giant (low/medium mass) or a red supergiant (high mass).

Chemical changes inside:

  • The core begins fusing helium into carbon.

Final Stage: The Star’s Death

Depending on mass after the explosion, it becomes a neutron star or a black hole.
